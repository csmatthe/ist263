Scoring

In the NFL, there are 4 ways to score points: touchdown, safety, extra point, and 2-point conversion. A touchdown is worth 6 points. It occurs when the offense gets the football in the endzone. The defense can also score a touchdown by taking away the football and running it into the other endzone. After a touchdown, the scoring team can choose a 2-point conversion or an extra point. An extra point is a 33-yard kick through the goal posts, worth 1 point. A 2-point conversion is an attempt to get the ball in the endzone again, worth 2 points. If these options fail, the scoring team gets only the touchdown points. A field goal, worth 3 points, happens when a team is close to scoring but runs out of plays. A safety, worth 2 points, occurs when the offense is tackled in their own endzone.

Downs

Understanding downs is crucial. The offense has 4 downs to move the ball 10 yards. Achieving this is a first-down. Typically, teams use 3 downs, reserving the 4th for a field goal or punt. A first down restarts where the previous play ended. For example, "2nd and 6" means it's the second down, and the team needs 6 yards. The defense aims to prevent the offense from converting a 1st down.

Different Plays

The offense can run countless plays, mainly involving running or passing. Predicting plays before the snap is challenging. When the QB holds the ball, it's likely a pass play; if given to the running back, it's a run play. Defense options include zone coverage, blitz, or man-coverage. Zone covers specific areas, blitz commits to the run, and man-coverage assigns defenders one-on-one. It's a high-stakes game, with various outcomes like drop passes, sacks, or interceptions.

Penalties

The NFL has an extensive rule book, but some basic penalties include false start, offsides, and offensive holding. False start (offense) and offsides (defense) involve movement before the snap, resulting in a 5-yard penalty. Offensive holding occurs when an offensive player holds a defender, moving the offense back 10 yards. Pass interference penalties, both offensive (OPI) and defensive (DPI), happen during pass plays due to excessive contact. OPI/DPI result in penalties and are signaled by the referee throwing a yellow flag.
